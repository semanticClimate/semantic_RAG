Foundations of climate science

This part introduces how Earth’s energy balance, greenhouse gases, and observations combine to explain climate change and climate change AND adaptation as a systems problem.

The greenhouse effect

The greenhouse effect is the process by which certain gases in the atmosphere trap heat, keeping the planet warmer than it would otherwise be. Without it, Earth would be far colder.

  • Incoming shortwave sunlight warms the surface.
  • The surface emits infrared radiation.
  • Greenhouse gases absorb and re‑emit some of that infrared.

Key greenhouse gases

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄) are central drivers of long‑term warming. Water vapour acts as a fast feedback.

Evidence from observations

Multiple independent lines of evidence — including temperature records, ocean heat content, and ice loss — show a warming world consistent with rising greenhouse‑gas concentrations.

Mitigation and adaptation

Mitigation reduces emissions or removes greenhouse gases from the atmosphere. Adaptation reduces harm from climate impacts we cannot fully avoid.

Energy and society

Energy systems link directly to emissions. Shifting toward renewable energy sources (solar, wind, hydro, sustainable bioenergy where appropriate) is a core mitigation strategy.
